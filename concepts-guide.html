Cluster 1: Real Assets vs. Financial Assets

Covers Questions: 1, 5, 15, 23

The Core Idea

Everything of value falls into two buckets:

Real assets are physical things that produce goods/services: land, buildings, machines, equipment, consumer durables (cars, fridges). They GENERATE wealth for the economy.

Financial assets are paper claims: stocks, bonds, commercial paper, derivatives. They ALLOCATE wealth -- they decide who gets what share of the income real assets produce.

How to Identify Which Is Which

Ask yourself: 'Can I use this to produce something or is it just a piece of paper claiming ownership?' If it produces = real. If it claims = financial.

Real: Land, buildings, production equipment, consumer durables, patents, software (as a product)

Financial: Common stock, bonds, commercial paper, options, futures

The Exchange Question (Q23)

When Surf City Software trades its software (a product it created = real asset) for Microsoft stock (a financial asset), it exchanged a REAL asset for a FINANCIAL asset. The software is real because it was created/produced. The stock is financial because it is just a claim on Microsoft's real assets.

⚠ EXAM TRAP!
Common stock is a FINANCIAL asset, not a real asset -- even though it represents ownership of a company that owns real assets. The stock itself is just a certificate. Production equipment IS a real asset.

Cluster 2: Types of Financial Assets

Covers Questions: 3, 4, 8, 17

Three Categories of Financial Assets

I. Debt Securities (Fixed Income): Bonds, T-bills, CDs, commercial paper, banker's acceptances. You lend money and get interest.

II. Equity Securities: Common stock, preferred stock. You own part of a company.

III. Derivative Securities: Options, futures. Contracts whose value depends on another asset.

All three categories (I, II, III) are financial assets. (Q8)

What Is NOT a Derivative? (Q3)

A share of common stock is NOT a derivative. It is direct ownership. Call options and futures ARE derivatives because their value is derived from the stock price.

What Is NOT a Money Market Security? (Q4)

Money market = short-term debt instruments (maturity under 1 year). T-bills, 6-month CDs, banker's acceptances qualify. Common stock has no maturity date and is equity, not debt -- so it is NOT a money market security.

Common Stock = Ownership (Q17)

A common stock represents an ownership share in a corporation. Not a call option (that is a contract), not a fixed-income security (that is debt), not preferred stock (different class of ownership).

⚠ EXAM TRAP!
Do not confuse money market securities with equity. Money market = SHORT-TERM DEBT ONLY. Stocks are equity, not debt, and have no maturity date.

Cluster 3: Primary vs. Secondary Markets

Covers Questions: 2, 6, 7, 14

Primary Market

Where NEW securities are sold for the first time. The company gets the money. Investment bankers help facilitate this process (underwriting, distribution).

Raising capital by issuing stock = primary market (Q7)

Purchasing a new issue = primary market + usually with investment banker assistance (Q6)

Secondary Market

Where PREVIOUSLY ISSUED securities are traded between investors. The company gets nothing from these trades. This includes transactions on stock exchanges (NYSE) AND in the OTC market (NASDAQ). (Q14: answer includes both venues)

Trading of previously issued stock = secondary market (Q2)

⚠ EXAM TRAP!
Q6: The quiz highlights just 'primary market' (b), but the most complete answer is (e) 'primary market AND usually with the assistance of an investment banker.' Investment bankers are involved in new issues. If the exam offers both options, pick the most complete one.

Cluster 4: The Players

Covers Questions: 9, 10, 11, 39, 40

Investment Bankers (Q10)

They are NOT banks. They do NOT take deposits or make loans. They do two things:

1. Act as intermediaries between companies issuing new securities and investors

2. Advise companies on financial needs and find buyers

Answer (d) combines both roles. Answer (c) describes commercial banks, not investment bankers.

Specialists / Designated Market Makers (Q9, Q40)

What they do: maintain limit order books, earn from commissions and spreads, stand ready to trade at bid/ask prices, and CAN trade in their own accounts.

Why they exist: to maintain a LIQUID and ORDERLY market (Q40). Not for competition, power, or SEC compliance.

Key: statements a, b, c are all true. Statement d ('cannot trade in own accounts') is FALSE. So the answer is 'all true EXCEPT d.'

Financial Intermediaries (Q11, Q21)

Commercial banks, insurance companies, AND investment companies are ALL financial intermediaries (Q11).

They exist because small investors cannot efficiently: diversify + gather information + monitor portfolios. ALL three reasons are valid (Q21).

Discount Brokers (Q39)

They offer little investment information and no advice (Q39: answer c). They just execute trades cheaply.

⚠ EXAM TRAP!
Investment bankers DO NOT accept deposits -- that is commercial banks. Specialists CAN trade in their own accounts. Do not confuse these roles.

Cluster 5: Investment Process

Covers Questions: 12, 20, 21, 34, 36

Asset Allocation vs. Security Selection

Asset Allocation (Q34): Deciding the broad split: 60% stocks, 30% bonds, 10% cash. This is TRUE when an investor decides to split funds between equities and bonds.

Security Selection (Q12): Choosing SPECIFIC securities within each class. Not the broad split -- the specific picks.

Active vs. Passive Management

Passive (Q20): Hold diversified portfolios without spending effort on analysis. Just track the market index.

Active: Spend resources researching, analyzing, and trying to beat the market.

The Efficient Markets Hypothesis (EMH) says active management is NOT the most appropriate strategy (Q36: FALSE). EMH favors passive management because prices already reflect all information.

Cluster 6: Order Types

Covers Questions: 18, 19, 26, 30, 31, 33, 35, 37, 38

Order Type Summary

Order TypeWhat It DoesWhen to Use
MarketBuy/sell immediately at best available priceWhen speed matters more than exact price
Limit-BuyBuy only at set price or LOWERGet a bargain -- wait for price to drop
Limit-SellSell only at set price or HIGHERSell at a good price or not at all
Stop-LossIf price DROPS to X, trigger a sellProtect gains on stocks you OWN

Question-by-Question Analysis

Q18 -- Protecting gains on a long position: You own JNJ stock at $50, now at $65. To protect gains, you need a STOP-LOSS order. The choices do not include stop-loss, so 'none of the above' (e) is correct.

Q19 -- Minimizing losses on a short sale: You shorted JCP at $80. If price rises, you lose money. To limit losses, you need a STOP-BUY order (c) that triggers a buy if price rises to a set level. The quiz incorrectly marks limit-buy (b).

Q26 -- Sell at or below specified price: Stop-loss (b). A stop-loss triggers a sell when the price drops to the stop price. Limit-sell does the opposite (sell at or ABOVE).

Q30 -- Ms. Brown's short position: Stock at $69, she wants to cover if it rises $1 to $70. She needs a buy stop order at $70 (b) -- it triggers a buy if price reaches $70.

Q31 -- Locking in profit: Joe bought 150 shares at $100. Wants $4,050 minimum profit. Minimum sell price = ($15,000 + $4,050) / 150 = $127. Place a stop-loss at $127 (b).

Q33 -- Limit orders buy at current price? FALSE (b). Limit orders specify a particular price. Market orders buy at the current price.

Q35 -- Limit-buy at $45 when stock is $50? TRUE (a). A limit-buy at $45 means 'buy only at $45 or lower.' The broker waits until the price drops.

Q37 -- Market order = specific exchange? FALSE (b). 'Market' in 'market order' means 'at the current market price,' not a specific exchange.

Q38 -- Stop order for volatile stock? TRUE (a). A stop order gives control over entry/exit price for unpredictable stocks.

⚠ EXAM TRAP!
Q19 ERROR: The quiz says limit-buy, but the correct answer is stop-buy. A limit-buy executes at or BELOW the limit price -- it cannot protect against a RISING price. Only a stop-buy triggers when the price reaches a level from below.

Cluster 7: Margin Trading Calculations

Covers Questions: 13, 22, 25, 29, 32, 41, 43, 44

Understanding Margin -- The Intuition

Buying on margin is like buying a house with a mortgage. You put up some of your own money (equity/margin), and the broker lends you the rest (loan). The key insight: the LOAN stays fixed, but your EQUITY changes as the stock price moves.

Formula 1: How Much Did You Borrow?

Intuition: If you put up X% of the cost, you borrowed (100% - X%).

AMOUNT BORROWEDTotal Cost = Shares x Price Per Share Your Money = Total Cost x Initial Margin % Loan (Borrowed) = Total Cost - Your Money = Total Cost x (1 - Initial Margin %)
Example (Q22) 200 shares at $70, initial margin 55%. Total cost = 200 x $70 = $14,000 Your money = $14,000 x 0.55 = $7,700 Borrowed = $14,000 - $7,700 = $6,300

Formula 2: What Is My Actual Margin Now?

Intuition: Your margin is the percentage of the current stock value that belongs to YOU (not the broker). As price goes up, your margin increases. As price goes down, your margin decreases. The loan never changes.

ACTUAL MARGINCurrent Stock Value = Shares x Current Price Your Equity = Current Stock Value - Loan Actual Margin = Your Equity / Current Stock Value
Example (Q29) 100 shares bought at $45, initial margin 70%. Price now $56. Loan = 100 x $45 x (1 - 0.70) = $1,350 Current value = 100 x $56 = $5,600 Equity = $5,600 - $1,350 = $4,250 Actual margin = $4,250 / $5,600 = 75.9%

Formula 3: At What Price Do I Get a Margin Call?

Intuition: A margin call happens when your equity drops to the maintenance margin percentage. You are asking: 'at what price does my ownership shrink to the bare minimum the broker allows?'

Start from: Actual Margin = Maintenance Margin

(Shares x Price - Loan) / (Shares x Price) = Maintenance Margin

Solve for Price: multiply both sides by (Shares x Price):

Shares x Price - Loan = Maintenance Margin x Shares x Price

Shares x Price x (1 - Maintenance Margin) = Loan

Price = Loan / [Shares x (1 - Maintenance Margin)]

MARGIN CALL PRICEPrice = Loan / [Shares x (1 - Maintenance Margin)] This makes intuitive sense: - Bigger loan = higher margin call price (more debt = more risk) - More shares = lower margin call price (spreading risk) - Higher maintenance margin = higher margin call price (stricter rules)
Example (Q13) 100 shares at $70, IM=50%, MM=30%. Loan = 100 x $70 x 0.50 = $3,500 Margin call price = $3,500 / [100 x (1 - 0.30)] = $3,500 / 70 = $50
Example (Q32) 100 shares at $45, IM=70%, MM=25%. Loan = 100 x $45 x 0.30 = $1,350 Margin call price = $1,350 / [100 x (1 - 0.25)] = $1,350 / 75 = $18

Formula 4: Rate of Return on Margin Purchase

Intuition: Your return is based only on YOUR money, not the total investment. It is simply: how much did your equity change?

RATE OF RETURNInitial Equity = Shares x Purchase Price x Initial Margin % Ending Equity = (Shares x Selling Price) - Loan Rate of Return = (Ending Equity - Initial Equity) / Initial Equity
Example (Q25) 300 shares at $60, IM=60%, sell at $45. Initial equity = 300 x $60 x 0.60 = $10,800 Loan = $18,000 - $10,800 = $7,200 Ending equity = (300 x $45) - $7,200 = $13,500 - $7,200 = $6,300 Return = ($6,300 - $10,800) / $10,800 = -41.67% Stock fell 25% but YOUR loss is 41.67% -- leverage amplified it!

True/False Concepts

Q41: Margin magnifies both gains AND losses (c). TRUE. It does NOT minimize losses. It amplifies everything.

Q43: Can buy more stock without cash by leveraging existing shares. TRUE. If your stocks rise, your excess equity can be used as collateral.

Q44: Margin call at initial margin? FALSE. Margin calls happen at the MAINTENANCE margin, which is LOWER.

⚠ EXAM TRAP!
Margin call is at MAINTENANCE margin, NOT initial margin. The initial margin is only required when you FIRST buy. After that, the maintenance margin is the floor. Also: margin does NOT 'minimize losses' -- it AMPLIFIES them.

Cluster 8: Short Selling Calculations

Covers Questions: 16, 27, 28, 45

Understanding Short Selling

Normal buying (going 'long'): you buy first, then sell later. You profit when the price goes UP.

Short selling: you SELL first (borrowed shares), then BUY later. You profit when the price goes DOWN.

Initial Investment in Short Sale

Intuition: When you short sell, the broker needs a deposit from you as insurance in case the stock goes up. Your deposit = the value of shares sold x your initial margin percentage.

INITIAL INVESTMENT (SHORT)

Sale Proceeds = Shares x Price Per Share Your Deposit = Sale Proceeds x Initial Margin % This deposit is YOUR initial investment
Example (Q16) Short 200 shares at $60, initial margin 60%. Sale proceeds = 200 x $60 = $12,000 Your deposit = 60% x $12,000 = $7,200

Profit/Loss on Short Sale

Intuition: You sold at a high price and want to buy back at a low price. Profit = what you sold for - what you bought back for.

SHORT SALE PROFIT/LOSS

Profit = (Sell Price - Buy-Back Price) x Shares If buy-back < sell: PROFIT (price went down as you hoped) If buy-back > sell: LOSS (price went up against you)
Example (Q28) Short 100 shares at $40. Price goes to $53. Close position. Result = ($40 - $53) x 100 = -$13 x 100 = -$1,300 This is a LOSS because you sold at $40 but had to buy back at $53.

Finding the Cover Price for a Target Profit

Intuition: You know how much profit you want. Work backwards to find the buy-back price.

COVER PRICE FOR TARGET PROFITTarget Profit = (Sell Price - Cover Price) x Shares Cover Price = Sell Price - (Target Profit / Shares)
Example (Q27) Short 120 shares at $150, want $2,400 profit. Cover Price = $150 - ($2,400 / 120) = $150 - $20 = $130 You must buy back at $130 or lower to make at least $2,400.

Q45: Broker acts as lender? TRUE. The broker lends you the shares (from their inventory or other clients' accounts) to sell short.

Cluster 9: Settlement and Trading Costs

Covers Questions: 24, 42

Q24 -- Trading Costs: Buying and selling stocks costs THREE things: (1) broker commissions, (2) dealer bid-ask spread, AND (3) possible price concession. Answer: (e) all three.

Q42 -- Settlement Period: Regular way settlement is T+3 (three business days), NOT one week. FALSE.

⚠ EXAM TRAP!
Settlement is T+3 (three business days), NOT one week. This is a common trick question.
